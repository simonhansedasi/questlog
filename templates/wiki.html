Knowledge Base

Concepts, edge cases, and how-tos for getting the most out of RippleForge.

New here? Start with the Quick Start guide instead →

What is RippleForge, exactly?

RippleForge is a causality engine for narrative. Its job is not to tell your story — it's to remember it accurately and propagate consequences automatically.

Entities Characters, factions, and locations in your world. The nodes in the graph.
Events Discrete things that happened — logged one at a time or extracted from notes. Events are the ground truth. Everything else is derived from them.
Relationships Computed from the pattern of events between entities. You don't set them — they emerge. A score that was Hostile three sessions ago drifts back toward Neutral if nothing negative has happened since.
Ripples Automatic consequences. When something happens to A, every entity connected to A feels it — allies share the impact, rivals feel the opposite. You log one event; the graph updates everywhere it should.

What it doesn't do

RippleForge never invents narrative. The AI proposes; the Narrator approves; nothing is written without a commit. It keeps score — you run the story.

Think of it as a referee who never forgets, never gets tired, and never loses track of who owes whom a favor. Every relationship score, every ripple event, every alert in the World State panel is derived from events you logged. The system is only as good as the events you put in.

Logging & Ripples

What do I log?

The threshold is simple: log something if this entity's standing meaningfully shifted. Don't log world flavor.

One question to ask before logging

Did something happen that changed how this entity is positioned in the world — or is this just scene description?

If it shifted their standing (positively, negatively, or in a way worth tracking) — log it. If it's atmosphere, skip it.

Don't log this

"The battle happened." — Pure scene description. Nothing and no one shifted.

Log these instead

"The general ordered the retreat." Negative on the general. "The alliance took heavy losses." Negative on the alliance. Each entity that was affected gets its own entry, with its own tone.

Multi-character scenes

Each entity that was meaningfully affected gets their own entry. A betrayal has at least two — what it cost the person betrayed, and what it says about the betrayer. Log both separately.

Background world events

A war breaks out. A city falls. A faction makes an announcement. Only log these against a specific entity if they change that entity's relationship to the observer. The war isn't an entry — "the Guild used the war as cover to seize the harbor" is an entry.

New characters

Don't pre-populate. Add entities when they appear in the story. Their first entry should be something that actually happened, not a description of who they are.

The AI parse handles this automatically when you paste notes — it extracts one entry per affected entity, not one entry per scene. Manual logging should follow the same pattern.

Logging an interaction — who goes in which field

The two fields that confuse everyone most are Who this affects and Caused by. Here's the rule:

Think subject → object

Caused by = who did it.  Who this affects = who it landed on.
Memory hook: "[Caused by] did it. It happened to [Who this affects]."

Worked example — Steve & Cheryl (neighborhood drama)

Beat 1 — Steve moves in
No actor. Who this affects: Steve. Caused by: nobody. Tone: neutral.
A world event — no relationship score yet.
Beat 2 — Cheryl brings cookies; Steve refuses to chat
Log this as two entries — one per side of the interaction:
• Who this affects: Cheryl / Caused by: Steve / tone: negative — the snub landed on Cheryl.
• Who this affects: Steve / Caused by: Cheryl / tone: neutral — Cheryl made a gesture Steve didn't want.
Beat 3 — Cheryl anonymously files a complaint against Steve
Who this affects: Steve / Caused by: Cheryl / tone: negative.
Mark Narrator-only if Steve doesn't know who filed it — the event exists and Cheryl caused it, but Steve only sees the consequence.

Two entries per interaction, not one. Real interactions have two sides — what Cheryl did lands on Steve, and Steve's response lands on Cheryl. Each is a separate entry with its own tone. This is how the system builds an accurate relationship score from both directions.

After a few interactions you'll see a computed relationship score appear on each character's page. When it's stable enough to be meaningful, RippleForge will prompt you to Formalize it as a graph edge — that's what wires them into the ripple propagation network.

Tone (polarity) — what it actually means

Tone describes whether this event was beneficial or harmful for this entity. Think of it as the direction the event pulled their standing — up, down, or sideways.

Positive — something beneficial happened to or because of this entity.

Examples: "Finch helped the group escape." "The Guild secured a major trade deal." The event reflects well on them or went in their favor.

Neutral — something happened worth recording, but their standing didn't shift meaningfully.

Example: "The Merchant Guild announced a new policy." Worth logging for the record, but not directional.

Negative — something harmful happened to or because of this entity.

Examples: "The bandits attacked the caravan." "Finch was captured." The event reflects badly on them or went against them.

How tone drives the relationship score

Positive events push an entity's score upward (toward Allied). Negative events push it downward (toward Hostile). Neutral entries are recorded but don't shift the score. Scores decay between sessions — an entity that hasn't had activity drifts toward Neutral.

Think of the score as this entity's standing in the world — how much weight they carry, in which direction.

Tone vs. "Caused by" — they're separate things

The Caused by field captures who did it. Tone captures how the event went. You can log "Bandits attacked the Guild" as a negative event on the Guild — with Caused by: Bandits — and both pieces of information are recorded separately.

When a tone event ripples through the graph, allies of the affected entity receive the same tone (their ally's loss is their loss); rivals receive the flipped tone (their rival's loss is their gain). This is automatic.

Reading the relationship score

Relationship scores are computed from your event history with time decay — recent events matter more. You can't manually set a score once events exist.

Label Score range What it means
Allied 75+ Strong sustained positive history. Would take significant risk for the observer.
Friendly 40-74 Warm relationship. Cooperative, no active conflict.
Neutral -39 to 39 No strong history either way, or history that has decayed.
Hostile -40 to -74 Active negative history. Distrustful, uncooperative.
At War -75 or lower Sustained antagonism. Would act against the observer given opportunity.

A score that was "Hostile" several sessions ago can drift back to "Neutral" if nothing negative has been logged recently — decay is intentional. Use it as a measure of active relationship temperature, not permanent reputation.

Revealing a secret (narrator-only → public)

When you log an event as Narrator-only, it's hidden from collaborators but visible to you everywhere — on character pages, in ripple traces, in the World State intel. Use it for secrets, hidden motives, and plot threads not yet revealed.

When the secret comes out

There's no one-click "reveal" — the original narrator-only event stays as-is (it's historical truth). Instead, log a new public event that represents the moment of revelation:

"The protagonists discover that Finch has been feeding information to the Merchant Guild all along."

This keeps the historical record clean (the original event was always true) while giving collaborators a logged moment of discovery they can see in the timeline.

Relation suggestions and backfilling

After parsing notes, the AI may suggest relationship edges your notes imply — e.g., "Finch and the Guild are connected." These appear below the Proposed Events section for you to accept or dismiss.

Accepting a suggestion adds the graph edge and backfills ripples — it retroactively fires ripple events to the newly connected character for every relevant polarity event in their history. This is intentional: if they were always connected, they would have always felt those ripples.

When to dismiss: if the suggestion is wrong or the connection is too weak to matter, dismiss it. Accepting a weak edge creates ripple noise for every future event. Fewer, meaningful connections are better.

Confirm vs. Dismiss a Pending Projection

Projections are committed "What happens next?" entries. They sit in the Pending Projections section of your dashboard, waiting for resolution.

Confirm

The event played out as projected. Confirming converts it to a real log entry and fires ripples to connected characters. Use this when the consequence happened — whether or not the protagonists were involved.

Dismiss

The protagonists intervened, or the situation resolved differently. Dismissing marks it as an intervention — no ripples fire. The projection is cleared from pending without becoming canon.

If neither applies yet — the projection is still in play — leave it pending. It stays visible in your intel layer as an active pressure.

Advanced Features

Branching timelines

Branches let you fork the world at any point and explore what happens differently — without touching the original timeline.

What branches share

All entities, relationships, and events that existed before the fork. Every branch starts from the same world state at the moment you created it.

What branches separate

Everything logged after the fork. Events on Branch A don't exist on Branch B or the main timeline. Each branch entry carries a tag in the event log so you can tell them apart.

How to create and switch branches

From the Narrator dashboard, use the Branch panel. Create a new branch and name it — the branch becomes active immediately. Everything you log goes to that branch. Switch back to the main timeline or another branch at any time from the same panel.

Deleting a branch removes all its events. The main timeline and other branches are unaffected.

When branches make sense

  • "What if the protagonists had taken the other path at the end of last session?"
  • Alternate histories — what if a key figure had survived, or a faction had held the city
  • Prep: sketch two versions of a scene before you know which way it goes
  • Exploring the downstream consequences of a decision before committing it

Branches are for exploration, not for staying. If you decide a branch is the real timeline, the cleanest path is to continue there — there's no "promote branch to main" operation. Keep the main timeline as your canonical one and use branches for exploration.

Dual-axis relationships

Every connection in RippleForge can have two axes:

Formal axis

What the relationship officially is. They signed a treaty. They're formally allied. The record says so.

Personal axis

What they actually feel. The treaty is signed. One side is quietly hostile. The official relationship and the real one are not the same.

When the two axes differ, the world graph shows two separate arcs between those entities — one per axis, colored differently. This is a visual signal that the relationship has tension that hasn't resolved yet.

How to add a dual-axis edge

From the Relations section on a character or faction card → Add Relation → set both Formal Relation and Personal Relation. If they're the same value, one arc renders. If they differ, two arcs render.

When to use it

  • A faction formally allied to another but harboring resentment after a betrayal
  • A character who treats someone professionally while privately despising them
  • Political marriages, enforced treaties, reluctant collaborations of any kind
  • Any situation where the official relationship and the emotional reality are pulling apart

The system doesn't resolve the tension automatically. It makes it visible. You decide when and how the divergence plays out in events — and when it does, you log it and the score shifts accordingly.

Mid-Story Situations

A character joins the protagonist group

A supporting character who already has a log history and graph connections is joining the protagonist group. You want them in that role without losing any of their existing context.

Steps

  1. Dashboard → scroll to World section → Add New Character → fill in their name and any relevant details. Add.
  2. Expand the new protagonist character card → Relations section → manually add the same connections the supporting character had (each character or group they were linked to, with the same relation type).
  3. Find the original character card → Narrator Controls → toggle Hidden. This removes them from the public world view but preserves their full event log as historical record.
  4. Log a brief narrator-only event on the original character: "Finch formally joins the protagonists." This bookmarks when the handoff happened in the timeline.
  5. From this point forward, log everything about this character against the protagonist character entry, not the supporting character entry.

Why not just delete the original entry? Their event log is historical record — ripples that fired from their events still reference that entity. Deleting it severs those traces. Hidden is safer.

Relations aren't auto-transferred. The original entry's graph edges live on that entry. You need to manually recreate the important ones on the protagonist character so their connections show up on the world graph going forward.

Stopped mid-scene — don't log what hasn't happened yet

You stopped in the middle of an unresolved scene. Nobody's fate is sealed yet. Don't log the scene as a conclusion — it hasn't concluded.

What to do

  • Paste a note into the Session Plan field on the dashboard capturing state: who's where, what's unresolved, any key details you need next session. This is your cheat sheet, not a parsed event.
  • Don't run Parse into Events yet — wait until the scene resolves so the AI sees the full arc in one pass.
  • When you resume, your notes can span both sessions. The parser handles multi-event blocks fine.

One exception: if something significant happened before the scene broke off (a betrayal, a revelation, a faction shift), Quick Log that now — you don't want it lost if context fades before next session.

A character dies

Whether it's a protagonist or a supporting character, death should be logged deliberately.

For a protagonist

  1. Quick Log a negative event on the character: "Ka-Ree dies." Set visibility to public if other characters witnessed it.
  2. Dashboard → expand the character card → Narrator Controls → toggle Hidden to retire them from the active protagonist display.
  3. Their log history and relations stay intact — ripples they received still trace back through their entity.

For a supporting character

  1. Log a negative event on the character: "[Name] is killed."
  2. Dashboard → character card → Narrator Controls → toggle Dead. This flags them in the UI and stops ripples from propagating to them automatically, without removing their history.

Mistakes & Corrections

Undoing a mistake

Logged something wrong? Two paths depending on whether it fired ripples.

No ripples involved (Quick Log, manual entry)

Find the entry on the character's page or in the dashboard log. Use the Delete Entry button (trash icon). Entry is removed, no ripple cleanup needed.

Entry fired ripples to other characters

Use Undo Ripple Chain on the source event. This removes all downstream ripple events that were generated from it, then you can delete the source entry. Find the option next to the event in the Ripples view or on the character's event log.

AI-parsed events committed by mistake: a commit just creates normal log entries — undo them the same way as above. There's no special AI-commit undo; entries are entries once committed.

Access & Collaborators

Sharing a read-only link with collaborators

Collaborators — co-authors, players, readers, research partners — don't need a RippleForge account to view a world. From the dashboard → Share section → copy the share link. Anyone with that link can see the public-facing world: public characters, groups, quests, journal entries.

What they can't see: anything logged as narrator-only, hidden entities, and anything marked restricted. The narrator PIN is never part of the share link.

Want collaborators to see only their character's perspective? Use the fog of war system instead — see below.

Per-character fog of war

Each protagonist character has a Known Events list. A collaborator assigned to that character only sees: (1) all public events, plus (2) any events you've explicitly added to their known list.

Setup

  1. Collaborators create a RippleForge account and log into the world with the share link.
  2. Dashboard → protagonist section → expand the character card → Assign User → type their username.
  3. When you log a restricted event and want a specific character to see it, add it to their Known Events list from the character card.

Use ?preview=CharacterName on any world URL to see it exactly as that character sees it — useful for checking before you share.

AI Features

Parse Notes — tips and gotchas

The parser reads raw notes and proposes discrete world events. It works best when you give it the full story, not just outcomes.

Notes can be rough

Bullet points, fragments, shorthand — all fine. The AI is extracting events, not grading prose. "Finch betrayed the group, ran to the Guild, protagonists chased, escaped through sewers" works.

New characters are auto-created as hidden

If your notes mention a name that isn't in your world, the parser will propose a new hidden character. Review and un-hide them from the dashboard if they're going to be recurring.

Polarity is about the observer, not the world

The AI uses polarity to mean "did the observer's relationship with this character improve or worsen?" — not "was this a good or bad event." Review polarity on each proposed entry before committing.

Uncheck before committing

Deselect any proposed event that doesn't belong before hitting Commit Selected. Committing is not undoable in bulk — you'd have to delete entries one by one.

Didn't like the parse? Don't commit — run it again with more context in your notes, or use Quick Log to manually record the events you care about.
